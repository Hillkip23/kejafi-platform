\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Real estate tokenization promises to democratize access to property investment, yet existing platforms 
lack rigorous, integrated research infrastructure spanning macro risk analysis, property valuation, 
and on-chain deployment. This paper presents \textbf{Kejafi}, a comprehensive three-stage platform 
that bridges institutional-grade quantitative finance with decentralized finance (DeFi) infrastructure. 
Stage~1 employs Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) processes and jump-diffusion models to forecast metro-level 
rent dynamics across Charlotte, Austin, and San Francisco, incorporating population growth and housing 
supply elasticity as key risk drivers. Stage~2 implements a rigorous Net Operating Income (NOI) 
valuation framework and deploys live ERC-20 token contracts (FINE5, FINE6) with Uniswap V3 liquidity 
pools on the Sepolia testnet. Stage~3 provides portfolio analytics including Monte Carlo simulation 
(10,000 scenarios), Value at Risk (VaR), Sharpe ratio, and a proprietary diversification score. 
Results demonstrate projected internal rates of return (IRR) of 12.3\% and 14.1\% for Charlotte 
and Austin properties respectively, with a combined simulated portfolio value of \$193,486 and a 
Sharpe ratio of 0.77. Population growth explains 73\% of long-term rent variation ($R^2 = 0.73$, 
$p < 0.01$), and geographic diversification reduces portfolio VaR by 32\%. Kejafi provides a 
replicable template for future real-world asset (RWA) tokenization platforms.

\vspace{0.4cm}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Real Estate Tokenization, Real-World Assets (RWA), Ornstein-Uhlenbeck 
Process, Monte Carlo Simulation, Uniswap V3, Value at Risk, Portfolio Management, DeFi
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

The global real estate market represents approximately \$280 trillion in assets---nearly three 
times the size of the global equity market and roughly 3.5 times global GDP---making it the 
largest single asset class in the world. Yet despite this extraordinary scale, real estate 
remains paradoxically one of the least liquid, most analytically opaque, and most inaccessible 
markets for retail investors. A retail investor can purchase a fractional share of Apple in 
seconds for \$1; purchasing a fractional share of an apartment building in Charlotte takes months 
and requires \$100,000 or more. Three structural inefficiencies define this landscape:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Illiquidity}: Properties typically require 3--6 months to sell, creating significant 
  capital lock-up periods that expose investors to substantial opportunity costs and market timing risk.
  \item \textbf{High Barriers to Entry}: Minimum direct investments routinely exceed \$100,000, 
  effectively excluding the retail investor segment from direct property ownership and its associated 
  return streams.
  \item \textbf{Analytical Fragmentation}: No standardized, integrated risk metrics exist for 
  tokenized real estate assets. Existing platforms provide transaction infrastructure but lack 
  rigorous quantitative underpinning.
\end{enumerate}

Real estate tokenization---the conversion of property ownership rights into blockchain-based 
digital tokens---offers a credible solution to each of these problems. By enabling fractional 
ownership, automated market makers (AMMs) provide continuous liquidity; transparent on-chain 
pricing replaces opaque bilateral negotiation; and smart contract governance replaces costly legal 
intermediation.

\subsection{Research Questions}

This paper addresses three primary research questions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item Can advanced stochastic models (OU processes, jump-diffusion) effectively capture and 
  forecast metro-level rent dynamics relevant to tokenized real estate valuation?
  \item What is the economic viability of end-to-end real estate tokenization, from property 
  acquisition through on-chain deployment?
  \item Do meaningful diversification benefits exist across tokenized real estate assets in 
  different metropolitan markets, and how should they be quantified?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}

Kejafi makes four primary contributions to the emerging real estate tokenization literature:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item A quantitative metro risk framework integrating population dynamics, supply elasticity, 
  and stochastic rent forecasting via the OU process.
  \item An end-to-end tokenization pipeline from property-level NOI modelling to live ERC-20 
  deployment with Uniswap V3 AMM liquidity.
  \item A portfolio analytics layer providing Monte Carlo VaR, Sharpe ratio analysis, and a 
  proprietary diversification score for tokenized real estate portfolios.
  \item An open-source, multi-stage Streamlit platform available for research replication 
  and live demonstration.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Real Estate Tokenization Platforms}

The real estate tokenization space has evolved rapidly since 2018, with several platforms 
pioneering fractional property ownership on blockchain infrastructure. \textbf{RealT} 
tokenizes individual residential properties with USDC-denominated revenue sharing distributed 
to token holders on a weekly basis. \textbf{Lofty AI} combines artificial intelligence for 
property selection with Algorand-based tokenization, lowering the minimum investment threshold 
to \$50. \textbf{Landshare} specialises in development-stage project tokenization on Binance 
Smart Chain. More recently, \textbf{Ondo Finance} has emerged as a significant player in the 
broader real-world asset (RWA) tokenization space, offering on-chain exposure to US Treasury 
bills and institutional-grade fixed income through its OUSG and USDY products 
\citep{ondo2024}. While Ondo's focus is fixed income rather than real estate, its architecture 
demonstrates the institutional appetite for yield-bearing tokenized assets and provides a 
template for compliant on-chain asset management at scale.

However, a consistent gap across all existing platforms is the absence of integrated 
quantitative research infrastructure. None provide a rigorous, transparent framework for 
metro-level risk analysis, stochastic rent forecasting, or portfolio-level VaR analytics. 
Kejafi is designed explicitly to fill this gap, combining the accessibility of retail 
tokenization platforms with the analytical rigour of institutional asset management.

\subsection{The Regulatory Landscape: CLARITY Act and Emerging Frameworks}

The regulatory environment for tokenized real-world assets is evolving rapidly and represents 
a critical determinant of the sector's long-run viability. In the United States, the 
\textbf{CLARITY Act} (Creating Legal Certainty for Entrepreneurs and Investors in the 
Digital Economy) has been introduced in Congress to establish a clearer jurisdictional 
boundary between the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Commodity Futures 
Trading Commission (CFTC) for digital assets \citep{clarity2024}. For real estate tokens 
specifically, the CLARITY Act's proposed framework would classify most property-backed tokens 
as securities, requiring compliance with existing Regulation D (private placement) or 
Regulation A+ (mini-IPO) exemptions for issuance to US investors.

This regulatory trajectory has important practical implications for platforms like Kejafi. 
Under a Reg D framework, token issuances would be restricted to accredited investors 
(net worth exceeding \$1 million or annual income exceeding \$200,000), limiting the 
democratization promise of tokenization. Reg A+ offers a broader investor base but imposes 
a \$75 million annual issuance cap and requires SEC qualification---a process that typically 
takes 3--6 months. Kejafi's current testnet deployment is explicitly designed with this 
regulatory pathway in mind, with the ERC-3643 permissioned token standard providing the 
on-chain compliance infrastructure for future mainnet issuance \citep{erc3643}.

\subsection{Industry Perspectives: Deloitte and Institutional Adoption}

Institutional analysis has increasingly validated the tokenization thesis. A widely cited 
Deloitte report on digital asset infrastructure identifies real estate tokenization as one 
of the highest-impact near-term applications of blockchain technology in financial services, 
projecting that tokenized real estate could represent a \$1.4 trillion market by 2030 
\citep{deloitte2024}. The report highlights three enablers of institutional adoption: 
(1) the maturation of permissioned blockchain infrastructure (Ethereum, Polygon, Avalanche); 
(2) the emergence of on-chain identity and KYC/AML solutions; and (3) the development of 
secondary market liquidity through AMM protocols such as Uniswap V3.

Deloitte further notes that the primary bottleneck is not technological but legal: the absence 
of standardised legal frameworks for the transfer of property ownership rights via blockchain 
transactions. Several US states, including Wyoming and Colorado, have passed legislation 
recognising blockchain-based property records, but federal-level clarity remains pending 
\citep{deloitte2024}. This institutional analysis reinforces the importance of Kejafi's 
testnet-first, compliance-aware development approach.

\subsection{International Context: Canada and Global RWA Developments}

The tokenization of real-world assets is not exclusively a US phenomenon. \textbf{Canada} 
has emerged as one of the most progressive jurisdictions for RWA tokenization, with the 
Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) issuing guidance in 2023 that explicitly 
recognises tokenized securities as falling under existing securities law, providing 
regulatory clarity that has attracted institutional experimentation \citep{csa2023}. 
Several Canadian real estate investment trusts (REITs) have explored tokenized unit 
issuance on the Ethereum and Stellar networks, and the Toronto Stock Exchange has 
engaged with blockchain-based settlement pilots.

Globally, the tokenized RWA market reached an estimated \$10 billion in on-chain value 
by early 2025, with real estate representing approximately 15\% of that total 
\citep{rwa2024}. Notable developments include Singapore's Project Guardian (a 
Monetary Authority of Singapore initiative testing tokenized asset markets), the 
European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation providing a harmonised 
framework for digital asset issuance across 27 member states, and the UAE's emergence 
as a hub for tokenized real estate with Dubai's Land Department piloting blockchain-based 
property registration.

These international developments suggest that the regulatory and technological 
infrastructure for mainstream tokenized real estate is converging, and that platforms 
demonstrating end-to-end viability---from quantitative research through on-chain 
deployment---will be well-positioned for the next phase of institutional adoption.

\subsection{Population Growth and Rent Dynamics}

A substantial body of real estate economics literature has established the link between 
metropolitan population growth and rent appreciation. \citet{glaeser2018} demonstrate that 
each 1\% increase in metropolitan population correlates with 0.4--0.6\% appreciation in 
real rents, with the relationship mediated by housing supply elasticity and local land use 
regulation. This finding underpins Kejafi's Stage~1 risk scoring model.

\subsection{Stochastic Modelling in Real Estate}

The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process has been extensively used in financial econometrics to 
model mean-reverting variables including interest rates \citep{vasicek1977}, commodity prices, 
and currency pairs. Its application to rental markets is natural given the well-documented 
tendency of rents to revert toward long-run equilibrium levels determined by population growth, 
income dynamics, and housing supply fundamentals.

Jump-diffusion extensions, following \citet{merton1976}, augment the continuous OU process 
with a compound Poisson process to capture discrete shocks arising from structural economic 
events---such as major employer relocations, natural disasters, or sudden regulatory changes---
that cannot be captured by Brownian diffusion alone.

\subsection{Real-World Asset Tokenization and DeFi}

Uniswap V3's concentrated liquidity architecture \citep{uniswap2024} represents the current 
standard for AMM-based token liquidity provision. Its application to real estate tokens 
introduces unique design considerations: lockup periods to prevent wash trading, fee tier 
selection commensurate with expected volatility, and liquidity range setting based on 
fundamental property valuation bounds. The Kejafi Stage~2 deployment addresses each of these 
considerations explicitly.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Stage 1: Metro Risk Analysis}

\subsubsection{Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Rent Dynamics}

Metro-level rent dynamics are modelled using the continuous-time Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process:

\begin{equation}
dX_t = \kappa(\theta - X_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t
\label{eq:ou}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $X_t$ is the rent level at time $t$, $\kappa > 0$ is the mean reversion speed 
(governing how rapidly rents return to equilibrium following a shock), $\theta$ is the long-run 
equilibrium rent level, $\sigma > 0$ is the instantaneous volatility parameter, and $dW_t$ is 
an increment of a standard Brownian motion under the physical measure.

The discrete-time Euler-Maruyama approximation used in simulation is:

\begin{equation}
X_{t+\Delta t} = X_t + \kappa(\theta - X_t)\Delta t + \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}\,\varepsilon_t, 
\quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)
\label{eq:ou_discrete}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Population Growth and Elasticity Adjustments}

Two metro-specific adjustments are applied to the base OU parameters:

\textbf{Population Growth Adjustment} (long-run mean):
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\text{adj}} = \theta + 0.5 \cdot g_{\text{pop}}
\label{eq:theta_adj}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Supply Elasticity Adjustment} (volatility):
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\text{adj}} = \sigma \times (1 + (0.30 - \varepsilon))
\label{eq:sigma_adj}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $g_{\text{pop}}$ is the 5-year metropolitan population growth rate and 
$\varepsilon \in (0,1)$ is the metro-specific supply elasticity. A market with elasticity 
below 0.30 will therefore exhibit higher adjusted volatility; one with elasticity above 0.30 
will exhibit lower adjusted volatility.

\subsubsection{Jump-Diffusion Extension}

Structural shocks are modelled by augmenting equation~\eqref{eq:ou} with a compound 
Poisson process:

\begin{equation}
dX_t = \kappa(\theta - X_t)\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t + J\,dN_t
\label{eq:jump_diffusion}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $N_t$ is a Poisson process with intensity $\lambda$ (expected jumps per year), 
and $J \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_J, \sigma_J^2)$ is the random jump size. Parameter values are 
estimated from historical Zillow Observed Rent Index (ZORI) data for each metro.

\noindent\textit{Note:} In the two-property demonstration (Charlotte, Austin), the jump-diffusion 
extension is calibrated but its contribution to the composite VaR is modest relative to the 
continuous OU component, as neither metro experienced a qualifying structural shock event within 
the 2015--2023 estimation window. The jump component becomes more material for markets with 
documented supply-side discontinuities (e.g.\ San Francisco post-2020).

\subsubsection{Kejafi Risk Score}

The composite Kejafi Risk Score $\mathcal{R} \in [0, 100]$ is constructed as a weighted 
combination of four sub-scores:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R} = w_1 S_{\text{pop}} + w_2 S_{\text{elast}} + w_3 S_{\text{vol}} + w_4 S_{\text{trend}}
\label{eq:risk_score}
\end{equation}

\noindent where the sub-scores normalise population growth, supply elasticity, rent volatility, 
and rent trend into $[0, 25]$ bands, and $(w_1, w_2, w_3, w_4) = (0.30, 0.25, 0.25, 0.20)$ 
are empirically calibrated weights. Higher scores indicate stronger risk-adjusted investment 
fundamentals.

\subsection{Stage 2: Property Tokenization}

\subsubsection{Valuation Framework}

The property valuation model follows a direct capitalisation approach. Net Operating Income 
is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\text{NOI} = (\text{Monthly Rent} \times 12 \times \text{Occupancy Rate}) - \text{Operating Expenses}
\label{eq:noi}
\end{equation}

Operating expenses include property taxes, insurance, maintenance reserves (typically 1--2\% 
of asset value annually), and property management fees (8\% of effective gross income). 
The stabilised property value is then:

\begin{equation}
V = \frac{\text{NOI}_{\text{stabilised}}}{\text{Cap Rate}_{\text{metro}}}
\label{eq:property_value}
\end{equation}

Cap rates are calibrated to metro-specific market data and are inversely related to supply 
elasticity: inelastic markets with higher asset prices command lower cap rates.

\subsubsection{Internal Rate of Return}

The 5-year IRR is computed by solving:

\begin{equation}
\sum_{t=1}^{5} \frac{CF_t}{(1 + \text{IRR})^t} + \frac{V_5}{(1 + \text{IRR})^5} - V_0 = 0
\label{eq:irr}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $CF_t$ is the annual NOI cash flow in year $t$, $V_5$ is the projected exit 
value assuming 2\% annual appreciation, and $V_0$ is the initial investment.

\subsubsection{Token Economics}

Each property is tokenised with 100,000 ERC-20 tokens. The initial token price is set at:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{token}} = \frac{V \times 0.80}{100{,}000}
\label{eq:token_price}
\end{equation}

The 0.80 factor reflects an 80\% collateralisation ratio, providing a 20\% equity buffer.

\subsection{Stage 3: Portfolio Management}

\subsubsection{Monte Carlo Simulation}

Portfolio returns $R_p$ are simulated over 10,000 scenarios using correlated normal shocks:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{R} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \mathbf{L}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, 
\quad \boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})
\label{eq:mc}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{L}$ is the Cholesky decomposition of the correlation matrix 
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$. Cross-asset correlations are set to $\rho = 0.50$ for same-metro 
assets and $\rho = 0.10$ for cross-metro assets, reflecting the localised nature of 
real estate market shocks.

\subsubsection{Value at Risk}

Parametric VaR at confidence level $\alpha$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\text{VaR}_\alpha = \inf\{x \mid P(L \leq x) \geq \alpha\}
\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}

\noindent The 95\% historical simulation VaR is taken as the 5th percentile of the 
Monte Carlo return distribution.

\subsubsection{Risk-Adjusted Performance}

The annualised Sharpe ratio is:

\begin{equation}
\text{SR} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[R_p] - R_f}{\sigma_p}
\label{eq:sharpe}
\end{equation}

\noindent using a risk-free rate of $R_f = 5.0\%$ (consistent with prevailing US 
Treasury yields as of early 2025).

\subsubsection{Diversification Score}

The proprietary Kejafi Diversification Score is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D} = \min\!\left(100,\; \frac{N_{\text{metros}} \times 20 + N_{\text{assets}} \times 5}{1 + H}\right)
\label{eq:div_score}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $H = \sum_i w_i^2$ is the Herfindahl-Hirschman concentration index 
computed on portfolio weights $w_i$, $N_{\text{metros}}$ is the number of distinct 
metropolitan markets, and $N_{\text{assets}}$ is the total number of tokenised assets.

\noindent\textbf{Note on VaR Estimation Methods:} Table~\ref{tab:portfolio_results} reports 
two VaR estimates that should be interpreted as complementary rather than contradictory. The 
\textit{parametric VaR} ($-8.2\%$) is derived from the analytical normal approximation

Monte Carlo scenarios visible in Figure~\ref{fig:mc}. The gap between the two ($\sim$4.6 
percentage points) reflects the well-known tendency of normal-distribution-based parametric VaR 
to underestimate tail risk in finite samples. The simulation-based estimate is the more 
conservative and appropriate figure for risk management purposes.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Interpretation of Key Findings}

The results provide strong empirical and simulation-based support for the three research 
questions posed in Section~\ref{sec:litreview}.

\textbf{RQ1 (Rent Forecasting):} The OU framework with population and elasticity adjustments 
successfully differentiates rent risk across metros. The $R^2 = 0.73$ for population growth 
as a predictor of long-term rent variation is consistent with \citet{glaeser2018}, while the 
elasticity-volatility relationship confirms that inelastic markets (San Francisco, $\varepsilon = 0.08$) 
exhibit rent volatility nearly double that of elastic markets (Austin, $\varepsilon = 0.48$).

\textbf{RQ2 (Economic Viability):} Both tokenised properties demonstrate attractive 
risk-adjusted returns, with IRRs of 12.3--14.1\% substantially exceeding the risk-free 
rate and the cost of tokenisation (\$145 in gas fees on testnet). The Sharpe ratio of 
0.77 compares favourably with broad equity indices, suggesting that tokenised real estate 
can serve as a viable alternative asset class within diversified portfolios.

\textbf{RQ3 (Diversification):} The 32\% VaR reduction from cross-metro diversification 
is economically significant. With only two assets, the diversification score of 50/100 
leaves substantial room for improvement through geographic expansion, suggesting that 
larger Kejafi portfolios spanning 5--10 metros could achieve diversification scores 
exceeding 80/100.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted when interpreting the results:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Data Constraints:} Historical ZORI data for Austin is available only from 
  2015, limiting the calibration window for OU parameter estimation.
  \item \textbf{Correlation Assumptions:} Cross-metro correlations are set exogenously 
  ($\rho = 0.10$) rather than estimated from data. In practice, correlations may increase 
  during stress periods due to common macro factors.
  \item \textbf{Oracle Dependency:} The valuation model relies on static cap rate and 
  population data inputs. Live on-chain deployment would require Chainlink oracle integration 
  for dynamic, tamper-proof data feeds.
  \item \textbf{Testnet Limitations:} All smart contract results are from Sepolia testnet. 
  Mainnet deployment would introduce additional considerations including regulatory compliance, 
  securities law, and real asset custody.
  \item \textbf{Jump Parameter Estimation:} Jump-diffusion parameters ($\lambda$, $\mu_J$, 
  $\sigma_J$) are calibrated from limited shock events in the sample window, introducing 
  estimation uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Directions}

The Kejafi roadmap identifies five priority extensions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Mainnet Deployment:} Transition from Sepolia testnet to Ethereum mainnet 
  with real asset backing, SEC Reg D or Reg A+ compliance, and institutional-grade custody.
  \item \textbf{Chainlink Oracle Integration:} Real-time rent, cap rate, and population 
  data feeds to enable dynamic token revaluation without manual data updates.
  \item \textbf{Machine Learning Enhancement:} Gradient boosting or LSTM models for 
  rent prediction, supplementing the OU framework with non-linear feature interactions.
  \item \textbf{Geographic Expansion:} Extension of the metro coverage to 50+ US markets 
  to enable higher diversification scores and more granular cross-metro comparison.
  \item \textbf{African Market Expansion:} Application of the Kejafi framework to East 
  African real estate markets (Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda), leveraging mobile-first 
  infrastructure and the region's growing tech-enabled property sector.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This paper has presented Kejafi, an end-to-end platform for tokenized real estate that 
integrates institutional-grade quantitative finance with live DeFi infrastructure. The 
platform's three-stage architecture---metro risk analysis, property tokenization, and 
portfolio management---addresses the core gaps identified in existing real estate tokenization 
platforms: the absence of rigorous analytical underpinning, standardised risk metrics, and 
portfolio-level diversification tools.

The key empirical and simulation results are as follows. Population growth explains 73\% of 
long-term rent variation across US metros ($R^2 = 0.73$), while supply elasticity is the 
primary determinant of rent volatility. The Charlotte (FINE5) and Austin (FINE6) properties 
yield projected IRRs of 12.3\% and 14.1\% respectively, with a combined simulated portfolio 
delivering a Sharpe ratio of 0.77 and a 32\% VaR reduction from geographic diversification. 
All smart contracts were successfully deployed and verified on Sepolia testnet at an 
all-in cost of approximately \$145.

Kejafi provides a replicable, open-source template for the next generation of real-world asset 
tokenization platforms. As regulatory frameworks for tokenized securities mature and on-chain 
oracle infrastructure becomes more robust, platforms like Kejafi are well-positioned to bridge 
the gap between traditional real estate investment and the emerging tokenized asset economy.

\section{OU Parameter Estimation}
\label{app:ou_params}

OU parameters are estimated via maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) on monthly ZORI data. 
For a discretised OU process the conditional likelihood is:

\begin{equation}
X_{t+\Delta} \mid X_t \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(X_t e^{-\kappa\Delta} + \theta(1 - e^{-\kappa\Delta}),\; 
\frac{\sigma^2}{2\kappa}(1 - e^{-2\kappa\Delta})\right)
\label{eq:ou_mle}
\end{equation}

Parameters are estimated by maximising the log-likelihood:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \arg\max_{\kappa, \theta, \sigma} \sum_{t=1}^{T-1} 
\log p(X_{t+1} \mid X_t;\, \kappa, \theta, \sigma)
\label{eq:mle}
\end{equation}
